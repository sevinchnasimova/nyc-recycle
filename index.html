New York City Recycling Information

NYC residents only recycle about 17% of their total waste, when they could be recycling almost double that amount with the current program.

Let's learn how we can get there.

Quick facts

Items that you *cannot* (surprisingly) recycle

  • soft paper like tissues and paper towels
  • plastic-coated or waxy paper
  • padded envelopes
  • soiled pizza boxes
  • sports balls
  • squeezable pouches
  • single-service food

How to properly recycle

  • determine which bin to recycle the item in. if you're unsure, play the Recycling Bin Game at the end of this page!
  • make sure to remove food + liquids

Ways to become more sustainable

  • walk or use public transporation
  • invest in a compost bin
  • donate unneeded items

Items that you can (surprisingly) recycle

  • mail envelopes
  • receipts
  • unsoiled parts of pizza boxes
  • wrapping papper
  • soft cover books
  • cardboard egg cartons
  • paper bags

Recent regulations about recycling and sustainablity

  • The Department of Sanitation of New York city issued a rule stating that all food-related businesses must be set out any solid waste in rigid receptacles with tight fitting lids. This change was made to reduce food sources of rats and clean up New York city.
  • The New York City Council voted to the Zero Waste Act which is a collection of laws that aim to advance the cityâ€™s efforts to divert organic waste from landfills, fight climate change, and create a greener and more sustainable future for New Yorkers. The five-bill legislative package codifies a mandatory residential curbside organics collection program, sets zero-waste targets for 2030, requires annual reporting on zero-waste efforts, creates community food scrap drop-off sites and establishes community recycling centers in the five boroughs. The Zero Waste Act addresses the over 11,000 tons of residential waste that New Yorkers produce daily and advances the city's efforts to fight the climate crisis.
  • The Bag-Waste Reduction Act was taken into effect because 23 billion plastic bags had been used each year. All plastic carry out bags are banned from distribution by anyone required to collect New York state sales tax. Many cities and counties were authorized to adopt a five-cent paper carry-out bag reduction fee.
  • New York State but a statewide ban on expanded polystyrene, single use foam food and beverage containers, and polystyrene loose filled packaging materials.
